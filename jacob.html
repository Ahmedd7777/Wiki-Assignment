A Brief History of the Analogue Stick

Introduction

Analogue sticks have been a ubiquitous part of video games for almost 30 years, but have you ever thought about the history of their conception? Starting in the 1970's several companies made use of rotating knob systems as a method for directional input in their games. While these controllers only worked in one dimension at a time- up or down, and left or right. Later, D-pads allowed for two dimensions of input, but with the advent of 3-D video games in the late 1980's, it became a necessity for players to be able to use a much larger range of directional inputs; Hence, the analogue stick!

Knobs & Paddles

Precursors to analogue sticks arrived in the form of rotating knobs on "paddle" controllers. Prominent examples include Atari's 2600 and 5200, ColecoVision, Intellivision, and Magnavox Oddysey. These knobs made use of potentiometers, allowing for a range of input signals in one dimension. Up and down for games like "Pong", or left and right for titles such as "Breakout" and "Space Invaders".
A collection of Atari systems and peripherals, including paddle controllers.

Non-analogue Advancements

By the 1980's games were quickly becoming more complex, and required more inputs to play. This meant additional buttons on the face of the controller, but more importantly, the D-pad. Allowing inputs for four cardinal directions and diagonals, the D-pad introduced a completely new form of video gaming. Since being brought into the spotlight by the Nintendo Entertainment System in 1983, all major video game controllers have featured a D-pad. Sony's playstation made use of a D-pad as it's only directional input, before upgrading to the Dualshock controller. The Nintendo 64, while being credited as the beginning of the analogue stick's prevelance, did not use potentiometers. Opting instead for a system of gears inside the stick's assembly, it operated much more like a trackball than any modern analogue stick we see today.

Playstation 1 controllers before and after the DualShock upgrade, and an NES controller with its famed D-pad.

Current Age

Although the technology has improved greatly, not much has changed in the world of analogue sticks for many years. In terms of design and innovation, things are largely the same as they were almost 30 years ago. Looking to the future, it's hard to say what could act as a replacement for such an integral part of gaming history.
